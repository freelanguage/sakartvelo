üìú Ancient History of Cannabis in Georgia

üè∫ The Scythian Connection (400 BCE)

The Caucasus Mountains, which form Georgia's northern border, have been home to cannabis culture for millennia. In 2013, archaeologists discovered 2,400-year-old golden vessels in a Scythian burial mound (kurgan) near Stavropol, just north of Georgia. These ornate gold artifacts‚Äîdescribed as "golden bongs"‚Äîcontained traces of both cannabis and opium residue.


The Scythians were nomadic warriors who roamed the Caucasus and Central Asian steppes. The ancient Greek historian Herodotus documented their cannabis rituals in the 5th century BCE, describing how they would throw cannabis seeds onto hot stones inside tents and inhale the vapors. The 2013 discovery confirmed his accounts and established the Caucasus region as one of the world's oldest centers of cannabis use.

üèîÔ∏è The Svan Cannabis Tradition

High in Georgia's Greater Caucasus mountains lies Svaneti, home to the Svans‚Äîan ancient Georgian ethnic subgroup living in some of Europe's highest settlements. The Svans developed a unique cannabis culinary tradition that persisted for centuries.


Local legend speaks of cannabis-infused khachapuri (Georgia's famous cheese bread) and other traditional dishes incorporating the plant. The Svans used cannabis not just recreationally but as part of their food culture, integrating it into their mountain cuisine. Soviet prohibition in the 20th century uprooted much of this tradition, but cultural memory persists in the region.


Research has identified Georgia and the broader Caucasus as part of an ancient "crop domestication center"‚Äîone of the world's regions where humans first cultivated plants including hemp and cannabis.

üåç Georgia: Crossroads of Cannabis Culture

Georgia's position at the crossroads of Europe and Asia made it a natural hub for cannabis distribution throughout history. The ancient Silk Road passed through the Caucasus, carrying goods‚Äîand plants‚Äîbetween East and West.


Hemp was cultivated throughout the region for fiber, rope, and textiles for thousands of years. Cannabis seeds have been found in archaeological sites across the South Caucasus dating back to the Bronze Age. The word "canvas" itself derives from "cannabis," reflecting the plant's historical importance in textile production‚Äîan industry Georgia participated in for millennia.

How activists, libertarians, and techno clubs changed Georgian drug policy forever

Organized protests emerge in Tbilisi demanding drug policy reform. Activists gather in central Tbilisi advocating for marijuana legalization, marking the beginning of a coordinated movement.

2015

The Girchi ("Pine Cone") party is founded by Zurab Japaridze and other libertarian activists who split from the United National Movement. Drug decriminalization becomes a core platform issue.

The White Noise Movement holds demonstrations outside Parliament calling for decriminalization of all drugs. The movement gains attention through creative, action-oriented advocacy rather than traditional lobbying.

Girchi Party activists plant 84 cannabis plants in pots at the party's Tbilisi headquarters as a political statement, directly challenging prohibition laws.

November 2017

Girchi member Givi Shanidze wins a petition before the Constitutional Court to lighten criminal liability for marijuana use. The court rules that existing laws impede personal freedom.

Prime Minister Giorgi Kvirikashvili resigns following the May protests, signaling the political impact of the reform movement.

üå± Home Growing: Current Status

‚ùå HOME CULTIVATION IS ILLEGAL

Despite the 2018 decriminalization of consumption, the Constitutional Court ruling explicitly kept in place penalties for cultivation. This means:


  • Growing any amount of cannabis at home is a criminal offense
  • There is no legal number of plants allowed for personal use
  • Penalties include fines and imprisonment
  • Indoor, outdoor, and greenhouse growing are all prohibited
  • Even growing hemp without proper licensing is illegal

‚ö†Ô∏è The Paradox

Georgian law creates an unusual situation: you can legally consume cannabis, but you cannot legally obtain it. Growing, buying, and selling all remain criminal activities. This forces consumers to rely on black market sources despite legal consumption rights.

üìä Penalties for Cultivation

Under Georgian law, cultivation of cannabis can result in:


  • Administrative fines for small-scale first offenses
  • Criminal prosecution for larger amounts or repeat offenses
  • Imprisonment up to 12 years for large-scale cultivation
  • Asset seizure of property used for growing operations

The June 2025 amendments have increased these penalties, making cultivation an even greater legal risk.

üîÆ The Future: Medical & Full Legalization

üíä Medical Marijuana: The Next Frontier

Unlike many Western countries, Georgia jumped directly to recreational decriminalization without first establishing a medical cannabis program. Activists and reformers are now pushing for:


  • A formal medical cannabis framework with patient registration
  • Licensed dispensaries providing regulated, quality-controlled products
  • Prescription access for patients with qualifying conditions
  • Research programs to study cannabis therapeutics

Currently, no medical marijuana program exists in Georgia. Patients seeking cannabis for medical purposes must rely on the same unregulated market as recreational users.

üåê Regional Context

Georgia's 2018 ruling made it the most progressive country in the former Soviet sphere regarding cannabis. However, the government has shown no indication of moving toward full commercial legalization. The June 2025 tightening of penalties suggests a conservative backlash rather than continued liberalization.

‚ö° What Needs to Change

For Georgia to develop a legal cannabis industry, lawmakers would need to:


  1. Legalize cultivation for licensed producers
  2. Create a regulatory framework for sales and distribution
  3. Establish quality and safety standards for products
  4. Implement taxation to generate government revenue
  5. Allow home growing for personal use within limits
  6. Decriminalize or legalize sales between adults

Girchi, White Noise, and allied organizations continue advocating for these changes, but political momentum has stalled since 2018.
